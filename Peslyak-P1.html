Model of Pathogenesis of Psoriasis: Part 1

Mikhail Peslyak

Edition e3.2 (+ editorial modifications)

Introduction
Baker 2000: Recent Advances in Psoriasis: The Role of the Immune System
The book introduces the hypothesis that "psoriasis is a disease of abnormal keratinocyte proliferation induced by T cells." Keratinocytes are the most common type of skin cell.
Iizuka 2004: Psoriatic architecture constructed by epidermal remodeling
The paper points to "epidermal hyperproliferation" as the direct cause of skin lesions (red, flaky, etc.) and identifies that when skin cells grow too quickly, they detach from the matrix material that connects skin tissue together, causing early cell death ("anoikis", i.e. a form of apoptosis or programmed cell death).
Weinstein 1985: Cell kinetic basis for pathophysiology of psoriasis
The paper's abstract notes that psoriatic cells go from a normal 311 hour lifecycle to 36 hours, and that the cell density of plaques is twice the number per millimeter. When combined, these factors correspond to a 28-fold increase in skin cell production.
 The self-renewal of the epidermis is a normal process: New cells are born in the basal layer; they mature, vary, and migrate toward the outside and form the external cornified layer; then, they die away and exfoliate. The standard lifecycle of an epidermal cell for areas of skin with average thickness (the renewal period) is 20-25 days. Psoriasis accelerates self-renewal—cells live only 4-10 days (Baker 2000, Iizuka 2004, Weinstein 1985). Cells migrating outside have no time to differentiate and they aren't quite functional. Psoriatic plaques have a red shade—they are tender, covered by white flakes due to the accelerated loss of cells, and they are much thicker.
Piruzian 2006: The molecular genetics of psoriasis
Original paper is written in Russian. The author analyzes many genes that may contribute to Psoriasis and concludes it is "determined by a combination of certain genes [relating to the immune system] as well as by various environmental trigger factors."
Giardina 2004: The psoriasis genetics as a model of complex disease
This paper was first to study the likelihood of psoriasis among human populations using statistical genetic analysis.
 Psoriasis isn't contagious. There are various types of psoriasis (codes are given according to ICD-10): vulgaris or plaque (L40.0), flexural or inverse (L40.83-4), erythrodermic (L40.85), pustular (L40.1-3, L40.82), guttate (L40.4). Chronic plaque psoriasis (CPs) is the most frequent type (more than 80% of total number of cases). Up to 15% of psoriatics also suffer from psoriatic arthritis (L40.5). Psoriasis is present in about 2-3% of the human population (120-180 million people). 4-6 million people are newly diagnosed with psoriasis every year. The disease can appear at birth or in extreme old age. Psoriasis is a chronic disease, so there are periods of aggravation and remission. Sometimes there appears to be no cause for periods of remission, and sometimes aggravation can be decreased as a result of treatment. Serious psoriasis can result in disability. The onset and progress of psoriasis is similar in men and women. African Americans, Indians, Chinese and Japanese suffer from psoriasis less frequently, however, and Eskimos don't suffer from psoriasis at all (Piruzian 2006, Giardina 2004).
Uniovular Twins
Also known as Monozygotic twins. The significance of this concordance is that, since monozygotic twins have precisely the same genes, over a large population of twins we can tell what percentage of a disease is genetic and what percentage is environmental.
Weisenseel 2002: Streptococcal infection distinguishes different types of psoriasis
Full paper here. "Streptococcal infection is confined to a particular subtype of non-pustular psoriasis, which is defined by early onset (< 40 years), a positive family history, and the HLA molecules HLA-Cw6, HLA-B13, or HLA-B57."
 Psoriasis is registered in the "Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man" as OMIM*177900. Psoriasis is a disease with a hereditary predisposition: concordance between uniovular twins is 70%. If one parent suffers from psoriasis, children are diagnosed with the disease in 15-25% of cases; if both parents suffer from psoriasis children are diagnosed with the disease in more than 40-60% of cases. The interrelation of allele HLA-Cw*0602 (chromosome 6p21) and psoriasis of the first type (characterized by its early beginning) has been demonstrated (Weisenseel 2002). This allele is found in more than 60% of psoriatics (not more than 15% of healthy people). Locuses of other chromosomes have weaker interrelations (Piruzian 2006, Giardina 2004). Psoriasis never manifests only in the presence of genetic defects—external exposure is necessary for the beginning and maintenance of psoriasis. Infections, skin traumas, stress, reaction to medications, climatic changes and other causes can provoke the onset of psoriasis or its aggravation (Molochkov 2007, Bos 2005, Fry 2007b, Gudjonsson 2004).
beta-hemolitic streptococci
A classification of streptococcus bacteria (it's a more specific variant of the bacteria that causes "strep throat"). The wikipedia article has a good explanation: basically, beta-hemolitic strep is known to be nasty not simply because of infection, but because its toxins seep into the body and wreak havoc on various organs and systems.
Molochkov 2007: Psoriasis and Psoriatic Arthritis
Original is in Russian. No English version appears to be available.
Khairutdinov 2007: Current views of immune mechanisms underlying the development of psoriasis
Original paper in Russian. It discusses the interaction between antigen-presenting cells, T-lymphocytes, and endothelial cells of skin vessels in the development of psoriatic rash. Special attention is given to the role of cytokines, chemokines, adhesion molecules, and growth factors. (From Google Translate).
Baker 2006a: Peptidoglycan and peptidoglycan-specific Th1 cells in psoriatic skin lesions
Strep cell walls are mostly made of peptidoglycan ("PG"), and based on research by this paper's authors, psoriatic skin cells have a higher percentage of PG than normal cells. In addition, the immune system's T cells are activated in both cases and triggers an attack on cells containing PG.
Baker 2003: Selective response of dermal Th-1 cells to 20-50 kDa streptococcal cell-wall proteins in chronic plaque psoriasis
This paper precedes the previous Baker paper, and identifies the (psoriasis) body's apparent confusion and unfortunate tendency to attack both strep cells and skin cells with its T cells.
Fry 2007b: Triggering psoriasis: the role of infections and medications
"Psoriasis can be provoked or exacerbated by a variety of different environmental factors, particularly infections and drugs. Strong evidence exists for the induction of guttate psoriasis by a preceding tonsillar Streptococcus pyogenes infection, whereas disease exacerbation has been linked with skin and/or gut colonization by Staphylococcus aureus, Malassezia, and Candida albicans." The paper also lists drugs that have an aggravating effect (or that may be responsible for provoking the disease).
Gudjonsson 2004: Immunopathogenic mechanisms in psoriasis
Explains a hypothesis for the interaction between immune cells (CD4+ and CD8+ T cells) that may cause psoriatic flare-ups and remissions of skin lesions. Also connects the HLA-Cw allele to theory and shows how its variant might be partially responsible for disease.
 Accelerated proliferation of keratinocytes (skin cells) is likely to be caused by erroneous actions of various antibacterial protection mechanisms of the skin. The influence of beta-hemolitic streptococci (first of all causing tonsillar infections) on the initialization and aggravation of psoriasis is avowed (Molochkov 2007, Khairutdinov 2007, Baker 2006a, Baker 2003, Baker 2000, Fry 2007b, Gudjonsson 2004).
etiology
The study of causation or origination.
pathogenesis
The mechanism by which a disease is caused.
Baltabaev 2005: Psoriasis and metabolism of bile acids
Original paper in Russian. Measurements of bile acid in people with psoriasis. (I think it's saying that bile acid components aren't breaking down properly in psoratic patients. The consequence would be that bacteria [aka microflora] in the gut are allowed to grow with fewer controls.)
Korotkii & Peslyak 2005: Psoriasis as a consequence of incorporation of beta-streptococci into the microbiocenosis of highly permeable intestines (a pathogenic concept)
This paper (by the same author) forwards the theory that hyperpermeability of the small intestine to certain proteins, coupled with beta streptococci in the gut, causes psoriasis. A "microbiocenosis" is an ecosystem of bacteria or microflora.
Baker 2006b: Peptidoglycan: a major aetiological factor for psoriasis?
This paper identifies how the body's innate immune system is probably responsible (at least in part) for psoriasis. Specifically, it appears that the genetically inherited "problem genes" that are associated with psoriasis are part of the "Peptidoglycan" (PG) recognizers built into our cells. Peptidoglycan is an "enemy protein" most commonly found in the cell wall of foreign bacteria such as streptococci. So if the PG recognizers are not specifically tuned for PG, there's a chance the body can erroneously target its own cells, which have similar traits.
Bos 2005: Psoriasis: dysregulation of innate immunity
This paper shifts the focus from psoriasis being a problem of the adaptive immune system only, to one of an innate immune system + adaptive immune system combination.
Danilenko 2008: Review paper: preclinical models of psoriasis
This paper is a meta-analysis of psoriasis models, and specifically covers mouse models of psoriasis ("murine mutant" models).
Gyurcsovics 2003: Pathophysiology of psoriasis: coping endotoxins with bile acid therapy
This paper introduces bile acid (dehydraulic acid) therapy and reports an 80% success rate in relieving psoriasis, using a 2-year follow up.
Lowes 2007: Pathogenesis and therapy of psoriasis
This paper is an overview of medical knowledge of psoriasis to date (2007).
Majewski 2003: Possible involvement of epidermodysplasia verruciformis human papillomaviruses in the immunopathogenesis of psoriasis: a proposed hypothesis
This paper introduces an interesting idea that implicates the EVHPV5 virus (which is normally benign). An increase in antibodies specific to EVHPV5 (which the psoriasis patients seem to produce) also cause skin cell growth ("proliferation").
Nestle 2005b: Defining Upstream Elements of Psoriasis Pathogenesis: An Emerging Role For Interferon Alpha
This is a comment on a paper, but the comment is not publicly available.
Nickoloff 2000: Is psoriasis a T-cell disease?
This paper is an overview of the scientific controversies regarding psoriasis (circa 2000), and points out that the disease (by eluding researchers) has spurred us to learn a great deal about autoimmune diseases in general.
Sabat 2007: Immunopathogenesis of psoriasis
This paper de-emphasizes the T cell as the major immune system component related to psoriasis and suggests that multiple stages of immune system cells are involved. Cites the efficacy of anti-TNF (tumor necrosis factor alpha) as evidence.
 There is no uniform point of view on etiology and pathogenesis of psoriasis. Researchers offer various models (Baltabaev 2005, Korotkii & Peslyak 2005, Khardikova 2000, Baker 2000, Baker 2006b, Bos 2005, Danilenko 2008, Gudjonsson 2004, Gyurcsovics 2003, Iizuka 2004, Lowes 2007, Majewski 2003, Nestle 2005b, Nickoloff 2000, Sabat 2007).

Objective
hepatobiliary system
The liver, gall bladder and bile ducts, and how they work together to make bile.
 This paper is a review and an analytical study of how the small intestine, hepatobiliary system, intestinal microflora and blood phagocytes interrelate in a dysfunctional way. The aim of the study is to substantiate a new model of psoriasis pathogenesis (i.e. furthering the Y-model). This model of pathogenesis of psoriasis was formulated in a previous work: Psoriasis as a consequence of incorporation of beta-streptococci into the microbiocenosis of highly permeable intestines (a pathogenic concept) (Korotkii & Peslyak 2005). It was based on colonization of the intestine by beta-hemolitic streptococci (BS), in association with the hyperpermeability of intestine walls for BS-proteins.
Boyman 2007: The pathogenic role of tissue-resident immune cells in psoriasis
This paper focuses on how T cells and dendritic cells cause psoriasis, but only at a local level (not a systemic level).
Boyman 2004:
An appreciable portion of results from new publications confirms this model; however, the results of some new studies of psoriasis (Baker 2006a, Baker 2006b, Boyman 2007, Boyman 2004, Clark 2006, De Jongh 2005, Fry 2007b, Gudjonsson 2004, Lande 2007, Majewski 2003, Nestle 2005a, Nestle 2005b, Ritchlin 2007, Sabat 2007, Gumayunova 2009a, Nesterov 2009) and mechanisms of cellular immune protection (Bachmann 2006, Blander 2006, Blander 2007, Buckley 2006, Fasano 2005, Medvedev 2006, Merad 2007, Myhre 2006, Noverr 2004, Sozzani 2005, Toivanen 2003) demanded specification, development and a more detailed formulation of the model.

Methods
anti-endotoxic
Endotoxins are toxins associated with Gram-negative bacteria, usually in certain bacteria's cell wall. Endotoxins trigger effects in the body which typically make you sick. So anti-endotoxic immunity (probably) refers to the body being able to express antibodies for the endotoxin and fight the bacteria that created it.
prepsoriatic skin
Skin that as yet shows no sign of psoriasis (no lesions, rash).
Medline: nlm.nih.gov
Contains journal citations and abstracts for biomedical literature from around the world.
Embase: embase.com
Contains Medline plus millions of other indexed records and thousands of mostly peer-reviewed journals.
SCSML: State Central Scientific Medical Library
Russian Ministry of Health central medical library

According to the website, it is the largest portal of Russian information in the field of science, technology, medicine and education. It contains full text and abstracts of more than 14 million scientific articles and publications.
 Any publication with a focus on the Gastrointestinal Tract (GIT) and hepatobiliary system, or that investigated the intestine and biliary microflora or anti-endotoxic and anti-streptococcal immunity in psoriatics were searched and analyzed. Attention was also given to publications that investigated prepsoriatic skin and an evaluation of the events initiating a psoriatic rash. Publications in the Medline and Embase databases, and Russian publications were searched in the State Сentral Scientific Medical Library and in the Scientific Electronic Library. The bibliography includes about 25% of the total number of analyzed publications. A review and/or analysis of the study results are given in the description of subprocesses. Any real or prospective mutual influence of subprocesses is given at the end of the description of dependent subprocesses.

Novelty and Hypotheses
Chemostatus
The range of chemokine receptors expressed by a cell (CCR1, CCR2, CCR3 etc.) including the amount of each. The chemostatus of a cell defines its behavior in reply to homeostatic and/or inflammatory chemokines (proteins secreted by cells).
R-phagocytes: "Reprogrammed phagocytes"
Phagocytes are white blood cells that eat harmful foreign particles. "Reprogrammed" in this context means they have two main properties:
  1. Despite contact, linkage with PAMP and endocytosis of PAMP (as a part of endocytosed F-content) their chemostatus similar to nonactivated
  2. Degradation of endocytosed F-content is slow and incomplete act so essential kPAMP part remains (kPAMP-carriage)
All tolerized phagocytes have Property 1. Property 2 depends on parity between rate of endocytosis F and intracellular proteolytic activity.
There are references where the subprocess has been proven and/or described in detail, or where the hypothesis has been summarized (see Appendix 3). The Systemic Psoriatic Process (SPP) and the hypothesis regarding the chemostatus of reprogrammed R-phagocytes are essentially new.
